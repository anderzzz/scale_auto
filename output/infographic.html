have more petrol stations?

Yes — but never in proportion. Across all of Sweden, fuel provision grows slower than the population it serves. One number captures how much slower; the places where that number fails turn out to map tourism, the Norwegian border, and the motorways.

The shape of it

Plot them, and the line bends

Take every Swedish municipality. Along the bottom, how many people live there (S); up the side, how many petrol stations it has (P). If stations simply tracked people, the dots would climb a straight ramp from the corner.

Make it exact

On log paper, the bend becomes a slope

Stretch both axes so that each step multiplies rather than adds — 10, 100, 1,000. The bent cloud falls onto a straight line. A straight line on log–log paper is a power law, P ∝ Sβ, and the whole story is now a single number: the slope, β.

$$P \;\propto\; S^{\beta}$$

If stations tracked people one-for-one, β would equal 1. A slope below 1 means each extra resident adds fewer stations than the last — the bend we just saw, written as one figure.

The surprise

Bigger places run leaner

Double a town’s population and its petrol stations grow by only about 20.59 ≈ 1.5×. Sweden fuels its large cities more efficiently, per person — the opposite of what “more people, more pumps” would suggest.

That is genuinely counter-intuitive, so the rest of this descent is spent trying to break it. We hand the doubter their two best objections, in turn, and watch what the slope does. Picture a wall at β = 1 — proportionality. The question for the next two scenes is simply: can anything push the slope to the wall?

Objection one — the denominator

“City people just own fewer cars”

Fair. A petrol station serves cars, not residents, and city-dwellers own fewer of them — transit, density, the price of parking. Maybe the efficiency is an accounting trick of the denominator. So we change it: count each municipality’s registered cars instead of its people. If car-ownership were the whole story, the slope would spring to the wall.

It moves — and stalls. Cars-per-person really do fall with size (0.60 in small municipalities, 0.49 in large), so about a fifth of the gap was the denominator. But the slope only climbs from 0.59 to 0.68 and stops well short of 1. Even counted per car, big places are served more efficiently. Objection one granted in full; the effect survives.

Objection two — the map

“Then you drew the boundaries wrong”

Also fair. “A place” is a choice. Redraw it — tight built-up settlements, whole municipalities, or entire commuting regions — and the slope shifts again. Here is every version at once, each with its uncertainty, all measured against the wall.

Why does the slope wander between 0.59 and 0.81? A municipality bundles a dense town together with its empty hinterland into one big-S parcel, which drags the slope down; a settlement measures only the built-up core. Same stations, different rulers — the textbook Modifiable Areal Unit Problem. There is no single “true” unit, so we state the unit with the number, and follow municipalities from here on because that is where the local stories live.

How we measured the slope honestly — count models, kept zeros, and the rootogram

Petrol stations are counted, not measured, and many small settlements have zero, one, or two. The textbook move — take logs and fit a straight line — cannot take the log of zero, so it silently drops every stationless place and biases the slope upward (for settlements, from 0.78 to a fake 0.46). We instead fit a Poisson / Negative-Binomial count model with a log link, which keeps the zeros and treats a town going from one station to two as the ordinary high-variance wobble it is, not a doubling.

The pivot

Now trust the line — and study what misses it

The slope held under every objection, so we stop measuring it and start using it. The fitted law becomes a quiet baseline; the dots that sit off it become the subject. A municipality with far more stations than its residents can explain is being asked a question: how big would you have to be to justify these pumps? Run the law backwards and the answer is the town’s effective population — the crowd of non-residents the pumps are really serving.

$$S_{\text{eff}}=\left(\tfrac{P}{C}\right)^{1/\beta}$$

The size that would make the observed station count most likely. The gap $S_{\text{eff}}-S$ is the implied crowd of ghosts: tourists, cross-border shoppers, transit traffic — demand the resident count never saw.

The same story, on the map

The misses cluster — it’s geography, not noise

Put every municipality where it actually sits, sized by population, coloured by how far it beats or misses the law — measured per registered car, so a big city near the line stays neutral rather than looking extreme. The surprises are not scattered at random. Over-stationed places line the Norwegian mountain border and the sparse north; under-stationed places ring the Stockholm and Göteborg suburbs.

This is the honest crux, so we say it plainly: no single municipality is a defensible outlier. Fourteen stray past the 95% mark — against 14.5 expected by pure chance — and none survives a multiple-testing correction. Rare things happen. What cannot happen by chance is the arrangement: the surprises clump hard (Moran’s I = 0.42, p = 0.001, a ~13σ departure from randomness). The structure is in the geography, never in any one dot.

Who are the ghosts?

Tourists — but only the kind that drive

If the extra stations serve visitors, the visitors should leave a footprint. Not the walk-around kind — a crowd at Stockholm’s Old Town arrives by metro and buys no fuel. The tourism that moves petrol arrives by car and stays: a ski week in Sälen, a camping trip on Öland. So we built the proxy deliberately from overnight beds and ski-lifts — and ignored the urban kind.

It lands. A municipality’s surplus tracks this mobile-tourism proxy at +0.58, far above the +0.20 from cheap unmanned pumps, the other rural suspect. And it is not just “rural places have both”: after stripping out residents, their cars and their income, the leftover surprise still follows tourism at +0.43 (p < 10⁻¹⁵).

Here is where the lens proves it sees more than one thing. The Norwegian-border municipalities are over-stationed and they are the big ski destinations — two stories tangled together. Switch the tourism proxy on, and it pulls them apart cleanly:

Pure ski resorts collapse to zero — the surplus was the skiers. The border-and-ski towns keep a stubborn residual: tourism erases about 57% of the Norway over-stationing, but a significant remainder survives (perm p = 0.006), and the Finnish border shows neither effect. That remainder is genuine cross-border fuel arbitrage — Norwegians driving over to fill up where tax is lower. One residual surface, two mechanisms, separated.

What the lens still can’t see — the Småland puzzle, brands, and income

The motorway cluster. With the mountains explained by tourism, the largest unexplained over-stationed group is interior Småland — Växjö, Alvesta, Ljungby. Not border, not high-tourism, and surviving cars, income and tourism. The likely culprit is through-traffic: the E4 and routes 25/27 carry freight and pass-through demand that neither resident cars nor overnight stays capture. Because tourism has already ruled out the obvious alternative, this is the clean next thing to test (Trafikverket traffic counts).

Brands sort by deviation. Manned majors (Circle K, OKQ8, St1) concentrate in the tight urban municipalities; unmanned automat chains (Din-X, Qstar) thicken the over-stationed rural tail. Real and significant, but a minor texture — the residual–automat correlation is only +0.2.

Income is a bundle, not a mechanism. The wealthy Stockholm-ring municipalities have fewer stations even per car, and income absorbs a quarter of that deficit — but income is inseparable here from land price and density, which is the very economics of sublinear scaling. A strong association; not proof that wealth zones out pumps.
